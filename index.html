Hostile Architecture

By: Alex DeHart

What is Hostile Architecture?

  • Hostile Architecture is not a new invention its been used for centuries. It was used as early as 1809 in London to prevent public urination.
  • In today's age, it's mostly seen implemented as metal knobs to prevent skateboarders from grinding on ledges, removal of public seating, and metal/concrete spikes protruding from the ground–so-called "anti-homeless spikes."

The London Spikes Controversy

This term originated from Twitter in 2014, following public outcry after spikes were added in alcoves at a London apartment to prevent sleeping of the homeless population. This term was popularized when activists highlighted the harsh, inhumane nature of the design. The spike removal petition received over 100,000 signatures.

Protests and Advocacy of Hostile Architecture

Hostile architecture has sparked significant public outcry and protests, particularly from homeless advocacy groups and activists. In response to the implementation of hostile architecture, many cities have seen demonstrations and campaigns aimed at raising awareness about the issue and advocating for more humane urban design.

Why It Matters Today

Hostile architecture is implemented to control the behavior of specific groups and protect property. However, it ultimately fails to solve homelessness—it only displaces it. Governments, corporations, and property owners use hostile architecture to maintain profits and public image rather than addressing systemic issues.

Impact on Homelessness

Hostile architecture worsens conditions for homeless individuals and criminalizes survival. Without places to rest, people experience health complications, sleep deprivation, and physical exhaustion. The design makes it difficult to access important services and increases risks from temperature extremes and injuries.

Moving Forward

Real solutions require addressing systemic causes: expanding affordable housing, strengthening social safety nets, and including affected communities in design decisions. Adaptive designs that accommodate everyone—rather than exclude—demonstrate how spaces can support rather than punish vulnerable populations.
